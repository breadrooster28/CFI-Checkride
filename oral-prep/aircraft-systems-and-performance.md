# Aircraft Systems and Performance

Total questions: 12

## 1. Describe the four-stroke cycle and why mixture matters at altitude.

- **ACS Area/Task:** Area II — Technical Subject Areas — Task C: Airplane Systems and Performance
- **Model answer:** Intake, compression, power, exhaust. As altitude increases, air density drops, so mixture often requires leaning to maintain proper fuel-air ratio and smooth power.
- **FAA reference:** PHAK Ch. 7; POH/AFM

## 2. What are common carburetor icing conditions and indications?

- **ACS Area/Task:** Area II — Technical Subject Areas — Task C: Airplane Systems and Performance
- **Model answer:** Can occur with visible moisture and moderate temperatures; symptoms include RPM drop in fixed-pitch props or manifold pressure drop/roughness. Apply carb heat per POH.
- **FAA reference:** PHAK Ch. 7; AFH emergency ops

## 3. How does electrical alternator failure usually present in light aircraft?

- **ACS Area/Task:** Area II — Technical Subject Areas — Task C: Airplane Systems and Performance
- **Model answer:** Low-voltage/ammeter indications, equipment brownout, and eventual battery depletion. Shed load, troubleshoot per checklist, and plan landing before total electrical loss.
- **FAA reference:** POH/AFM; AFH emergency procedures

## 4. Explain why density altitude degrades takeoff and climb performance.

- **ACS Area/Task:** Area II — Technical Subject Areas — Task C: Airplane Systems and Performance
- **Model answer:** Higher density altitude reduces engine power, propeller thrust, and wing lift while increasing true airspeed for a given indicated speed, lengthening takeoff and reducing climb rate.
- **FAA reference:** PHAK Ch. 11; AFH performance

## 5. What performance factors must be included before every takeoff?

- **ACS Area/Task:** Area II — Technical Subject Areas — Task C: Airplane Systems and Performance
- **Model answer:** Weight and balance, runway length/condition/slope, pressure altitude, temperature, wind, obstacles, and POH/AFM performance data with safety margin.
- **FAA reference:** 14 CFR 91.103; POH/AFM

## 6. How do you explain VA to a student?

- **ACS Area/Task:** Area II — Technical Subject Areas — Task C: Airplane Systems and Performance
- **Model answer:** VA is design maneuvering speed at a given weight, not a guarantee against all structural damage; abrupt full control deflection above VA can exceed limits, and VA decreases with weight.
- **FAA reference:** PHAK Ch. 9; POH/AFM
- **Gotcha / DPE follow-up:** Gotcha: “below VA = always safe” is incorrect.

## 7. What is the difference between Vx and Vy, and when would each be used?

- **ACS Area/Task:** Area II — Technical Subject Areas — Task C: Airplane Systems and Performance
- **Model answer:** Vx gives best angle of climb for obstacle clearance; Vy gives best rate of climb to gain altitude fastest. Both vary with altitude and aircraft condition.
- **FAA reference:** AFH Ch. 10; POH

## 8. A student wants to depart at max gross on a hot afternoon from a short runway. How do you decide?

- **ACS Area/Task:** Area II — Technical Subject Areas — Task C: Airplane Systems and Performance
- **Model answer:** Run full POH performance calculations with conservative margins, evaluate abort points and alternatives, and decline operation if margins are inadequate.
- **FAA reference:** POH/AFM; 14 CFR 91.103
- **Scenario-based:** Yes

## 9. What is required for legal and practical fuel planning for VFR day and night?

- **ACS Area/Task:** Area II — Technical Subject Areas — Task C: Airplane Systems and Performance
- **Model answer:** Legal minimum under 14 CFR 91.151 is 30 minutes reserve day and 45 minutes night at normal cruise; practical planning should include extra reserve for delays and contingencies.
- **FAA reference:** 14 CFR 91.151
- **Gotcha / DPE follow-up:** DPE follow-up: “What reserve do you personally teach?”

## 10. How do center-of-gravity limits affect controllability?

- **ACS Area/Task:** Area II — Technical Subject Areas — Task C: Airplane Systems and Performance
- **Model answer:** Forward CG raises stall speed and increases required tail-down force/landing distance; aft CG reduces stability and can make stall/spin recovery harder.
- **FAA reference:** PHAK Ch. 9; POH/AFM

## 11. What does “known icing approval” mean for GA trainers?

- **ACS Area/Task:** Area II — Technical Subject Areas — Task C: Airplane Systems and Performance
- **Model answer:** Most trainers are not approved for known icing. Pilots must avoid known icing conditions and exit inadvertent icing immediately using published procedures.
- **FAA reference:** POH/AFM limitations; AIM guidance

## 12. How would you teach systems-based troubleshooting after rough engine in cruise?

- **ACS Area/Task:** Area II — Technical Subject Areas — Task C: Airplane Systems and Performance
- **Model answer:** Maintain aircraft control, use checklist (carb heat/mixture/fuel selection/ignition), assess nearest suitable landing, and brief contingency if power loss occurs.
- **FAA reference:** AFH emergency operations; POH
- **Scenario-based:** Yes
