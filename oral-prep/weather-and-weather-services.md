# Weather and Weather Services

Total questions: 10

## 1. What weather sources satisfy 14 CFR 91.103 preflight responsibilities?

- **ACS Area/Task:** Area II — Technical Subject Areas — Task D: Weather Information
- **Model answer:** All available information relevant to the flight, including weather reports/forecasts, fuel requirements, alternatives, known delays, and runway data.
- **FAA reference:** 14 CFR 91.103; AIM 7

## 2. Decode this METAR element: “BKN015 OVC030 2SM -RA BR.”

- **ACS Area/Task:** Area II — Technical Subject Areas — Task D: Weather Information
- **Model answer:** Broken at 1,500 ft AGL, overcast at 3,000 ft AGL, 2 statute miles visibility, light rain and mist. Conditions are marginal and possibly IFR depending on airspace/class.
- **FAA reference:** AIM 7; Aviation Weather resources

## 3. How do TAFs differ from METARs for planning?

- **ACS Area/Task:** Area II — Technical Subject Areas — Task D: Weather Information
- **Model answer:** METAR reports observed conditions; TAF provides forecast trends with change groups (FM, TEMPO, PROB where applicable). Use both for go/no-go decisions.
- **FAA reference:** AIM 7; Aviation Weather Handbook

## 4. What does an AIRMET Sierra/Tango/Zulu indicate?

- **ACS Area/Task:** Area II — Technical Subject Areas — Task D: Weather Information
- **Model answer:** Sierra: IFR/mountain obscuration; Tango: turbulence/strong winds; Zulu: icing/freezing level hazards.
- **FAA reference:** AIM 7; Aviation Weather Handbook

## 5. When would a SIGMET be especially critical for GA training flights?

- **ACS Area/Task:** Area II — Technical Subject Areas — Task D: Weather Information
- **Model answer:** Convective SIGMETs (embedded/severe thunderstorms) and non-convective SIGMETs for severe icing/turbulence/dust storms/volcanic ash indicate high-risk conditions often unsuitable for training.
- **FAA reference:** AIM 7

## 6. Explain basic frontal weather patterns a CFI should teach.

- **ACS Area/Task:** Area II — Technical Subject Areas — Task D: Weather Information
- **Model answer:** Cold fronts often bring convective activity and gusts; warm fronts bring widespread stratiform clouds/precipitation; occluded/stationary fronts can create prolonged poor conditions.
- **FAA reference:** PHAK Ch. 12; Aviation Weather Handbook

## 7. A student proposes “scud running” under a lowering ceiling to get home. How do you respond?

- **ACS Area/Task:** Area II — Technical Subject Areas — Task D: Weather Information
- **Model answer:** Reject the plan, explain CFIT and VFR-into-IMC risk, evaluate alternates/diversions, and use ADM models to choose conservative options.
- **FAA reference:** PHAK Ch. 2; NTSB safety themes
- **Scenario-based:** Yes

## 8. How do winds aloft forecasts influence cross-country planning?

- **ACS Area/Task:** Area II — Technical Subject Areas — Task D: Weather Information
- **Model answer:** They affect groundspeed, fuel burn, ETA, and altitude selection; evaluate multiple altitudes to optimize performance and reserve margins.
- **FAA reference:** PHAK Ch. 16; XC planning guidance

## 9. What is a temperature-dewpoint spread and why does it matter?

- **ACS Area/Task:** Area II — Technical Subject Areas — Task D: Weather Information
- **Model answer:** Small spread indicates high relative humidity and fog/low cloud potential, especially around sunset/sunrise and light winds.
- **FAA reference:** PHAK Ch. 12

## 10. How should a CFI brief convective weather avoidance minimums?

- **ACS Area/Task:** Area II — Technical Subject Areas — Task D: Weather Information
- **Model answer:** Maintain large stand-off distances, avoid flight under anvils, anticipate outflow boundaries/microbursts, and never rely on penetrating cells in light GA aircraft.
- **FAA reference:** AIM thunderstorm guidance; Aviation Weather Handbook
