# Principles of Flight and Maneuver Aerodynamics

Total questions: 14

## 1. Why does induced drag decrease as airspeed increases in level flight?

- **ACS Area/Task:** Area II — Technical Subject Areas — Task B: Principles of Flight
- **Model answer:** Higher airspeed requires lower angle of attack for the same lift, reducing strength of wingtip vortices and induced drag.
- **FAA reference:** PHAK FAA-H-8083-25 Ch. 5

## 2. What causes adverse yaw and how do you teach correction?

- **ACS Area/Task:** Area II — Technical Subject Areas — Task B: Principles of Flight
- **Model answer:** Down-going aileron increases drag on the rising wing, yawing opposite roll direction. Teach coordinated rudder with aileron and verify with slip/skid indicator and outside references.
- **FAA reference:** PHAK Ch. 5; AFH Ch. 4

## 3. Explain load factor changes in a level steep turn and stall-speed effect.

- **ACS Area/Task:** Area II — Technical Subject Areas — Task B: Principles of Flight
- **Model answer:** As bank increases in level flight, load factor rises and stall speed increases by the square root of load factor. At 60° bank, load factor is about 2G and stall speed increases substantially.
- **FAA reference:** PHAK Ch. 5; AFH performance maneuvers
- **Gotcha / DPE follow-up:** DPE follow-up often asks for the 60°/2G relationship.

## 4. In a chandelle, when is maximum pitch attitude reached and why?

- **ACS Area/Task:** Area II — Technical Subject Areas — Task B: Principles of Flight
- **Model answer:** At about 90° of turn, then bank is gradually reduced while maintaining increasing pitch to minimum controllable airspeed by rollout heading. It demonstrates coordinated energy management.
- **FAA reference:** AFH FAA-H-8083-3 Ch. 9; CFI ACS

## 5. What are key errors in lazy eights from an aerodynamic standpoint?

- **ACS Area/Task:** Area II — Technical Subject Areas — Task B: Principles of Flight
- **Model answer:** Poor coordination, incorrect pitch/bank timing at 45°/90°/135° points, and inability to reach minimum airspeed at 180° with proper heading/altitude control.
- **FAA reference:** AFH Ch. 9; CFI ACS

## 6. Explain pivotal altitude for eights on pylons.

- **ACS Area/Task:** Area II — Technical Subject Areas — Task B: Principles of Flight
- **Model answer:** Pivotal altitude approximates groundspeed squared divided by 11.3 (knots version) and changes with wind/groundspeed. Pitch adjustments keep the pylon visually fixed by changing line-of-sight geometry.
- **FAA reference:** AFH Ch. 9
- **Gotcha / DPE follow-up:** Gotcha: use groundspeed, not indicated airspeed.

## 7. Differentiate an aerodynamic stall from low airspeed.

- **ACS Area/Task:** Area II — Technical Subject Areas — Task B: Principles of Flight
- **Model answer:** A stall is exceeding critical angle of attack, regardless of indicated airspeed, attitude, or power setting.
- **FAA reference:** PHAK Ch. 5; AFH slow flight/stalls

## 8. What is the proper spin awareness teaching emphasis for CFI applicants?

- **ACS Area/Task:** Area II — Technical Subject Areas — Task B: Principles of Flight
- **Model answer:** Teach stall/spin relationship, recognition, prevention, and recovery concepts using POH/AFM limitations; avoid intentional spins unless aircraft and instructor are approved and current for spin training where required.
- **FAA reference:** 14 CFR 61.183(i); AFH Ch. 4; POH/AFM

## 9. How do you explain slow flight objective under current ACS/AFH guidance?

- **ACS Area/Task:** Area II — Technical Subject Areas — Task B: Principles of Flight
- **Model answer:** Slow flight teaches recognition and management near critical AOA with coordinated control, proper trim/power use, and avoidance of stall warning trends while maintaining specified performance.
- **FAA reference:** AFH Ch. 4; Airman Certification Standards

## 10. Why does left-turning tendency increase at high power and low airspeed?

- **ACS Area/Task:** Area II — Technical Subject Areas — Task B: Principles of Flight
- **Model answer:** Torque, P-factor, spiraling slipstream, and gyroscopic effects are more pronounced when power is high and aerodynamic control effectiveness is lower.
- **FAA reference:** PHAK Ch. 5; AFH

## 11. A student repeatedly overshoots rollout heading in steep turns. What aerodynamic and instructional corrections do you apply?

- **ACS Area/Task:** Area II — Technical Subject Areas — Task B: Principles of Flight
- **Model answer:** Likely poor anticipation and overbanking with delayed coordination. Teach lead rollout, stable bank reference, coordinated rudder, and smooth elevator back-pressure for altitude hold.
- **FAA reference:** AFH Ch. 9; AIH critique methods
- **Scenario-based:** Yes

## 12. What is accelerated stall risk during maneuvering flight?

- **ACS Area/Task:** Area II — Technical Subject Areas — Task B: Principles of Flight
- **Model answer:** High load factor can cause stall at speeds above 1G stall speed, especially in steep turns or abrupt pull-ups. Avoid abrupt control inputs and teach maneuvering speed limitations.
- **FAA reference:** PHAK Ch. 5; AFH

## 13. How do slips affect aerodynamics and landing use?

- **ACS Area/Task:** Area II — Technical Subject Areas — Task B: Principles of Flight
- **Model answer:** Forward slip increases drag and descent rate with cross-controlled inputs; side slip aligns longitudinal axis with runway centerline in crosswind landing.
- **FAA reference:** AFH Ch. 8

## 14. Why can go-arounds from full-flap configurations become hazardous?

- **ACS Area/Task:** Area II — Technical Subject Areas — Task B: Principles of Flight
- **Model answer:** Pitch-up tendency, drag, and configuration changes demand prompt directional and pitch control with proper flap retraction schedule and obstacle awareness.
- **FAA reference:** AFH Ch. 8; POH procedures
